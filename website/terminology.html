📖 Trading Terminology

Definitions for every term used across the Live Trading and Market Summary dashboards — organized by category. Use the search box or category filters to find any term instantly.

🔍
📊 Chart & Price Action
Candlestick
A chart type showing a bar's Open, High, Low, and Close prices. A green (bullish) candle means Close > Open; a red (bearish) candle means Close < Open. The wicks show the High and Low extremes of the period.
Both Pages
OHLCV Open · High · Low · Close · Volume
The five standard data points for each bar: Open (first trade price), High (highest price), Low (lowest price), Close (last trade price), and Volume (number of shares/contracts traded).
Both Pages
Volume
The total number of shares or contracts traded during a bar period. Displayed as a histogram below the candlestick chart. Green bars = up-close bars; red bars = down-close bars. Higher volume confirms price moves.
Both Pages
ATR Average True Range
Measures average bar-to-bar price volatility over 14 periods. True Range = max(High−Low, |High−PrevClose|, |Low−PrevClose|). Used to set stop losses at 1.5× ATR and to validate that magnitude forecasts exceed 1× ATR before taking a trade.
Live Trading
ATR % ATR as % of Price
ATR divided by the current closing price, expressed as a percentage. Normalizes volatility across different-priced assets — a $5 ATR on a $500 stock (1%) has very different meaning than on a $50 stock (10%).
Live Trading
Signal Marker
An arrow displayed directly on the candlestick chart at historical BUY/SELL signal points generated by the ML model. ▲ green arrow below bar = BUY signal; ▼ red arrow above bar = SELL signal. Includes the probability percentage.
Live Trading
Bar / Candle
A single time-period unit on the chart. Each bar represents the full OHLCV data for that interval (e.g., one 1H bar covers one hour of trading). The ML models train on historical bars and predict the direction of the next bar.
Both Pages
OC Range Open-to-Close Range
The percentage change from Open to Close within a single bar: (Close − Open) / Open. A positive OC Range means a bullish bar; negative means bearish. Used as a ML input feature capturing intra-bar sentiment.
Live Trading
HL Range High-to-Low Range
The percentage spread from High to Low within a bar: (High − Low) / Open. A wide HL Range indicates high intra-bar volatility. Used as a ML feature to capture how much price swung during the period.
Live Trading
📈 Technical Indicators
RSI Relative Strength Index
A momentum oscillator (0–100) measuring the speed and magnitude of price changes over 14 periods. Above 70 = potentially overbought; below 30 = potentially oversold. One of the top features in the ML model across all asset types.
Both Pages
MACD Moving Average Convergence Divergence
Calculated as EMA(12) − EMA(26). Positive MACD = short-term momentum above long-term trend (bullish). The MACD Signal Line is a 9-period EMA of the MACD. The MACD Histogram = MACD − Signal, showing momentum acceleration.
Both Pages
MACD Histogram
The difference between the MACD line and its Signal line (MACD − Signal). Positive and growing = accelerating bullish momentum. Crossing zero from below = potential buy signal. Used as a standalone ML feature to detect momentum shifts.
Both Pages
Bollinger Bands
Bands plotted 2 standard deviations above and below a 20-period SMA. The ML model uses two derived features: BB Width (band spread / midline — measures volatility) and BB Position (where price sits within the bands: 0 = lower, 1 = upper).
Both Pages
BB Width Bollinger Band Width
(Upper Band − Lower Band) / SMA20. Low BB Width = price coiling in a tight range (potential breakout ahead). High BB Width = price already in a volatile expansion. Used by ML as a volatility context feature.
Both Pages
BB Position
(Close − Lower Band) / (Upper Band − Lower Band). A value near 1 means price is at the top of the bands (overbought zone); near 0 means at the bottom (oversold zone). Helps the ML model identify mean-reversion setups.
Both Pages
Stochastic %K 14-Period Stochastic
Compares the current Close to the High–Low range over 14 periods: (Close − Low14) / (High14 − Low14) × 100. Values above 80 suggest overbought conditions; below 20 suggest oversold. Captures where price is within its recent trading range.
Both Pages
Williams %R
Similar to Stochastic but inverted: −(High14 − Close) / (High14 − Low14) × 100. Range: −100 to 0. Readings near 0 = overbought; near −100 = oversold. Used by the ML stock and options models to identify reversal zones.
Live Trading
ADX Average Directional Index
Measures trend strength (not direction) on a 0–100 scale. ADX > 25 = strong trend; < 20 = weak/ranging market. Used in the futures and index ML models (replacing OBV, which is unreliable for futures). Higher ADX = momentum trades work better.
Both Pages
OBV On-Balance Volume
A cumulative indicator adding volume on up-bars and subtracting it on down-bars. The ML model uses the 10-bar OBV slope (normalized to ±1) to capture whether volume is flowing into or out of an asset. Used for stocks and options only — unreliable for futures/indices.
Live Trading
SMA Ratio SMA20 / SMA50 / SMA200 Ratio
Close price divided by its 20-, 50-, or 200-period Simple Moving Average. A ratio > 1 means price is above the average (bullish); < 1 means below (bearish). The 200 SMA ratio is a long-term trend filter; 20 and 50 capture shorter momentum.
Both Pages
EMA Exponential Moving Average
A moving average that gives greater weight to recent prices. Used internally to compute MACD (EMA12 − EMA26) and the MACD Signal Line (EMA9 of MACD). Reacts faster to recent price changes than a Simple Moving Average.
Both Pages
HV5 Ratio Historical Volatility Ratio
5-bar realized volatility divided by 20-bar realized volatility (both measured as rolling standard deviation of returns). A ratio > 1 means short-term volatility is elevated relative to longer-term — used in options models to detect volatility spikes.
Live Trading
Vol Ratio Volume Ratio
Current bar's volume divided by its 20-bar average volume. A Vol Ratio > 1.5 indicates unusually high activity. Used to rank assets by trading activity on the Market Summary page and as an ML feature for momentum confirmation.
Both Pages
🤖 ML Models & Techniques
GBM Gradient Boosting Machine
An ensemble of decision trees trained sequentially, where each tree corrects the errors of the previous one. The dashboard uses GradientBoostingClassifier (direction) and GradientBoostingRegressor (magnitude) from scikit-learn. Parameters: 200 estimators, max_depth=4, learning_rate=0.05, subsample=0.8.
Both Pages
ExtraTrees Extremely Randomized Trees
An ensemble of decision trees where both the feature and the split threshold are chosen randomly at each node — faster than GBM and less prone to overfitting on small datasets. Combined with GBM in a voting ensemble for improved direction predictions.
Live Trading
Ensemble / Voting Classifier
Combines multiple models and averages their probability predictions (soft voting) to produce a final signal. The dashboard uses VotingClassifier([GBC, ExtraTrees]) for direction and VotingRegressor([GBR, ExtraTreesRegressor]) for magnitude — ensemble reduces variance vs. a single model.
Live Trading
Cross-Validation CV · 5-Fold Walk-Forward
A technique to estimate how well a model generalizes to new data. The dashboard uses TimeSeriesSplit(n_splits=5) — a walk-forward approach that always trains on past data and tests on future data, respecting the temporal ordering of markets. Reported as CV Accuracy.
Live Trading
Walk-Forward Validation
A backtesting methodology that simulates real trading: train on data up to time T, test on T+1, then roll the window forward. This prevents look-ahead bias (using future data to predict the past). More reliable than random cross-validation for time series.
Live Trading
Feature Importance
How much each input variable (feature) contributes to the model's predictions. Calculated from the GBM's split gain at each node. Displayed in the Top Feature Drivers panel and the ML Ranking section — helps explain why the model made a specific decision.
Both Pages
Feature Engineering
The process of transforming raw OHLCV data into meaningful inputs for the ML model. Includes computing RSI, MACD, ATR ratios, SMA ratios, return windows (1, 3, 5, 10, 20 bars), vol regime, OBV trend, and stochastics. The dashboard computes 18–23 features depending on the asset type.
Both Pages
Per-Asset-Type Models
The dashboard trains separate ML models with different feature sets for Stocks (23 features), Options (18 features, volatility-heavy), Futures (21 features, ADX-based trend), and Indices (same as Futures). This improves accuracy vs. one-size-fits-all models.
Both Pages
StandardScaler
A preprocessing step that standardizes each feature to have mean = 0 and standard deviation = 1 before feeding data to the ML model. Prevents features with large ranges (e.g., volume) from dominating features with small ranges (e.g., RSI). Applied to both training and prediction data.
Live Trading
Multi-Horizon Forecast
Separate GBM models trained to predict returns over 1 day, 1 week, and 1 month horizons using daily bars. Each horizon has its own model, P(UP) probability, and target price. Available for SPY, QQQ, ES=F, and GC=F on the Market Summary page.
Market Summary
Soft Voting
An ensemble strategy where each model outputs a probability vector and the final prediction is the weighted average of probabilities (vs. hard voting which just takes majority class votes). Soft voting produces better-calibrated probabilities and is used for the direction classifier.
Live Trading
CV Accuracy
The average classification accuracy across the 5 walk-forward cross-validation folds. Reported on the signal panel. A CV Accuracy ≥ 57% is used as a quality gate — signals from models below this threshold should be avoided. Random chance = 50%.
Live Trading
🎯 Signals & Thresholds
P(UP) Probability of Upward Move
The model's probability (0.0–1.0) that the next bar will close higher than the current bar. Displayed as a percentage on the probability gauge. A BUY signal requires P(UP) ≥ 60% (62% in high-volatility regimes). Computed by the Voting Classifier's soft probability output.
Both Pages
P(DOWN) Probability of Downward Move
Equal to 1 − P(UP). The model's probability that the next bar will close lower. A SELL signal requires P(DOWN) ≥ 60% (i.e., P(UP) ≤ 40%). Displayed on the left side of the probability gauge opposite P(UP).
Both Pages
BUY Signal
Issued when P(UP) ≥ 60% (or ≥ 62% in high-volatility regime). Displayed as a green badge and an upward arrow marker on the chart. Suggests a long position — buy the asset or open a bullish options/futures position following the trade setup panel's entry, stop, and target levels.
Both Pages
SELL Signal
Issued when P(UP) ≤ 40% (or ≤ 38% in high-volatility regime). Displayed as a red badge and a downward arrow marker on the chart. Suggests a short position — short the asset or open a bearish options/futures position following trade setup levels.
Both Pages
HOLD Signal
Issued when P(UP) is between 40% and 60% (38%–62% in high-vol). The model does not have sufficient directional conviction. Do not trade. Wait for a higher-probability setup to develop before entering a position.
Both Pages
Confidence Threshold
The minimum P(UP) or P(DOWN) probability required to issue a BUY or SELL signal. Standard: 60% BUY / 40% SELL. High-volatility regime: 62% BUY / 38% SELL. Tighter thresholds in high-vol reduce false signals when markets are choppy.
Live Trading
Volatility Regime Vol Regime
Classifies the current market environment as Low (0), Normal (1), or High (2) volatility by comparing the current ATR% to its 50-bar rolling median. High regime = ATR% > 1.5× median; Low regime = ATR% < 0.75× median. Affects thresholds, signal interpretation, and trade sizing.
Both Pages
Magnitude Forecast Pred. Magnitude %
The predicted percentage price move for the next bar, output by the GBM Regressor. Positive = bullish move, negative = bearish move. Used to set price targets (Entry + Magnitude) and validate the signal: magnitude should exceed 1× ATR to be worth trading.
Both Pages
Probability Gauge
A visual bar on the signal panel showing the split between P(DOWN) on the left and P(UP) on the right. The fill color shifts from red (bearish) through yellow (neutral) to green (bullish) based on the model's output probability.
Live Trading
Signal Quality Checklist
A six-point validation framework for each signal: (1) P(UP) ≥ 65% or P(DOWN) ≤ 35% (2) Magnitude ≥ 1.0× ATR (3) CV Accuracy ≥ 57% (4) Threshold filter applied (5) Risk defined (1.5× ATR stop) (6) Target at magnitude forecast. All six should be met before entering a trade.
Live Trading
Signal Breadth
The percentage of all tracked assets (stocks + options + futures + indices) currently showing a BUY signal vs. a SELL signal. High breadth (≥60% BUY) = broad market bullish environment; low breadth (≥60% SELL) = broad bearish pressure. Displayed on the Market Summary overview cards.
Market Summary
Market Direction
The aggregate classification of all signals: BULLISH (≥60% BUY), SLIGHTLY BULLISH (45–60% BUY), NEUTRAL, SLIGHTLY BEARISH, or BEARISH (≥60% SELL). Computed from all unique symbols in the market summary batch run.
Market Summary
⚙️ Trade Setup
Entry Price
The price at which you open a position. In the trade setup panel, entry = current Close price at the time of the signal. For limit orders, you may set the entry slightly below (BUY) or above (SELL) the current price to improve fill quality.
Live Trading
Stop Loss Stop · 1.5× ATR
The price at which you exit to limit losses. Set at 1.5× ATR away from entry: BUY stop = Entry − 1.5×ATR; SELL stop = Entry + 1.5×ATR. Using ATR ensures the stop is calibrated to the asset's actual volatility — not too tight (stops out prematurely) or too wide (too much risk).
Live Trading
Target Price Take Profit
The profit-taking level: Entry ± Magnitude Forecast %. BUY target = Entry × (1 + magnitude%). SELL target = Entry × (1 − |magnitude%|). The target represents the ML model's predicted price after one bar's move. Only trade when target distance exceeds the stop distance (R:R > 1).
Live Trading
R:R Ratio Risk:Reward Ratio
Target distance divided by stop distance: (Target − Entry) / (Entry − Stop). An R:R of 2:1 means you risk $1 to potentially make $2. The dashboard displays the computed ratio — a signal should have an R:R ≥ 1.5 to be worth taking after accounting for commissions and slippage.
Live Trading
Position Sizing
How many shares/contracts to trade. Standard rule: risk no more than 2% of account equity per trade. Shares = (Account × 2%) / (Entry − Stop). For futures, risk per contract is displayed directly — divide your 2% dollar limit by the risk per contract to determine lot size.
Live Trading
Reward per Unit
The dollar profit expected per share (stocks) or per point (futures) if the target is reached: |Magnitude%| × Entry Price. For futures this is then multiplied by the contract multiplier to get the dollar reward per contract.
Live Trading
Slippage
The difference between the expected fill price and the actual fill price — caused by the bid-ask spread and market impact. For liquid ETFs (SPY, QQQ) slippage is minimal. For less liquid stocks or fast-moving futures, slippage can meaningfully erode edge — factor it into R:R calculations.
Live Trading
🎯 Options Trading
IV Implied Volatility
The market's expectation of future price volatility, derived from current options prices. High IV = expensive premiums (good for selling options); Low IV = cheap premiums (good for buying options). Always check IV rank before selecting an options strategy.
Live Trading
IV Rank
Where current IV sits relative to its 52-week range: (Current IV − 52w Low) / (52w High − 52w Low) × 100. IV Rank < 30 = buy premium strategies (debit spreads). IV Rank > 60 = sell premium strategies (credit spreads, iron condors).
Live Trading
Debit Spread
An options strategy where you buy one option and sell another at a different strike, paying a net debit (upfront cost). Used when IV is low and you want to buy cheap premium with limited risk. Examples: Bull Call Spread (bullish), Bear Put Spread (bearish).
Live Trading
Credit Spread
An options strategy where you sell one option and buy another further away, collecting a net credit (premium received upfront). Used when IV is high. Maximum profit = credit received; maximum loss = spread width minus credit. Examples: Bull Put Spread, Bear Call Spread.
Live Trading
Bull Call Spread
Buy a call at a lower strike, sell a call at a higher strike (same expiration). Bullish strategy with limited risk and limited reward. Use when IV Rank < 30 and the ML model shows a BUY signal. Maximum profit when price exceeds the short call strike at expiration.
Live Trading
Bear Put Spread
Buy a put at a higher strike, sell a put at a lower strike (same expiration). Bearish strategy with limited risk. Use when IV Rank < 30 and the ML model shows a SELL signal. Maximum profit when price falls below the short put strike at expiration.
Live Trading
Bull Put Spread
Sell a put at a higher strike, buy a put at a lower strike — collect a net credit. Bullish credit spread. Use when IV Rank > 60 and ML shows BUY. You profit if the underlying stays above the short put strike. Risk = spread width minus credit received.
Live Trading
Bear Call Spread
Sell a call at a lower strike, buy a call at a higher strike — collect a net credit. Bearish credit spread. Use when IV Rank > 60 and ML shows SELL. You profit if price stays below the short call strike. Maximum loss = spread width minus credit received.
Live Trading
Iron Condor
Simultaneously sell a Bull Put Spread and a Bear Call Spread — a neutral, range-bound strategy. Profit when the underlying stays between both short strikes. Use when IV Rank > 60 and the ML signal is HOLD (no directional conviction). Maximum profit = combined credit received.
Live Trading
Strike Selection
Choosing the option strike prices for a spread. Dashboard rule: the break-even price of the spread must fall inside the ML model's predicted magnitude move. If the model predicts +2% and your break-even requires +3%, the spread is too wide — choose closer strikes.
Live Trading
Break-Even
The underlying price at which an options position has zero profit or loss at expiration. For a Bull Call Spread: lower strike + net debit paid. Your break-even must be inside the ML model's predicted magnitude move for the trade to have positive expected value.
Live Trading
📡 GEX & Options Flow
GEX Gamma Exposure
The aggregate net gamma that all options market makers (dealers) must hedge in the underlying asset. Computed as the sum of gamma × open interest × contract size × spot price across all strikes and expiries. Positive GEX = dealers are net long gamma (stabilizing). Negative GEX = dealers are net short gamma (amplifying). The single most important structural indicator for near-term S&P 500 behavior.
Market PrepForecast
Gamma Wall
The strike price with the largest concentration of positive dealer gamma. Acts as a strong magnetic resistance ceiling — dealers must aggressively sell the underlying as price approaches this level to maintain delta-neutrality, capping upside. In a long-gamma regime, price often oscillates below the Gamma Wall for days to weeks. Breaking through it triggers a short-squeeze acceleration.
Market PrepForecast
Put Wall
The strike price with the largest concentration of put open interest and associated dealer long gamma. As price falls toward the Put Wall, dealers must buy large quantities of the underlying to hedge their short delta from the puts — creating a powerful mechanical support floor. Institutions often place protective puts at round numbers, making the Put Wall highly predictable and self-fulfilling.
Market PrepForecast
Gamma Flip Level Zero Gamma Line
The underlying price at which aggregate dealer gamma crosses from positive to negative (or vice versa). Above the flip = Long Gamma Regime (dampening). Below the flip = Short Gamma Regime (amplifying). This level is the most critical intraday decision point — a sustained break below it shifts dealer behavior from stabilizing to momentum-amplifying. Displayed in the stats strip on the Market Prep and Forecast pages.
Market PrepForecast
Long Gamma Regime GEX Positive
When dealers are net long gamma (spot price above Gamma Flip level). Dealers sell rallies and buy dips to maintain delta-neutrality — dampening realized volatility. Markets become range-bound with low realized vol. Characteristics: slow grind higher, dips bought aggressively, vol crushed. Best strategies: iron condors, credit spreads, selling premium, mean-reversion fades. Typical during: low-VIX environments, post-OpEx calm periods.
Market PrepForecast
Short Gamma Regime GEX Negative
When dealers are net short gamma (spot price below Gamma Flip level). Dealers buy rallies and sell dips — amplifying every move. Realized volatility exceeds implied volatility. Characteristics: trending directional moves, stops triggered in cascades, vol spikes. Best strategies: momentum trend-following, buying debit spreads, long straddles. Typical during: fear regimes, earnings seasons, macro events, VIX > 20.
Market PrepForecast
Delta Hedging
The continuous process by which options dealers buy or sell the underlying asset to maintain a delta-neutral portfolio. When a dealer sells a call, they must buy shares to hedge. As price moves, delta changes (due to gamma), forcing re-hedging. This systematic, non-fundamental buying and selling is the largest mechanical force in US equity markets — estimated at billions of dollars per 1% SPY move. Delta hedging creates the structural support (Put Wall) and resistance (Gamma Wall) levels visible on GEX charts.
Market PrepForecast
Vanna
The rate of change of delta with respect to implied volatility: ∂Delta/∂IV. When IV falls (a "vol crush" post-event), dealer deltas change, forcing large hedging flows in the underlying. Vanna flow is why equities often rally for 2–5 sessions after an event that causes IV to crush (earnings, FOMC, CPI). A 5-point IV drop on SPY can force billions in systematic buying via vanna unwinding. One of the three major higher-order Greeks driving systematic non-fundamental price moves (alongside Charm and Speed).
Market PrepForecast
Charm Delta Decay
The rate of change of delta with respect to time: ∂Delta/∂t. As options approach expiry, their deltas migrate toward 0 (OTM) or 1 (ITM) — forcing dealers to unwind hedges. Charm flow is strongest in the final 90 minutes of a 0DTE trading day, as thousands of near-expiry options rapidly change delta. Post-OpEx charm unwind (week 4 of each month) is often the cleanest trending week as vanna + charm hedges unwind simultaneously and dealers go gamma-flat.
Market PrepForecast
Flow Sentiment
An aggregated measure of bullish vs. bearish options premium flow for a given asset. Computed from the relative volume and premium of call vs. put activity: BULLISH = call premium dominates; BEARISH = put premium dominates; NEUTRAL = balanced. Displayed in the stats strip on the Market Prep page and as a column in the option flows table. Unlike PCR (which measures volume), Flow Sentiment weights by premium paid — capturing institutional intent more accurately than raw volume counts.
Market Prep
PCR Put/Call Ratio
Total put volume divided by total call volume for a given asset over the trading session. PCR > 1.2 = bearish hedging dominant — historically a contrarian bullish signal (market is overly hedged). PCR < 0.7 = call speculation dominant — contrarian bearish signal (complacency). Displayed in the options flow table on the Market Prep page. Equity PCR and index (SPX) PCR often diverge — index PCR is more useful for macro hedging signals.
Market PrepForecast
Max Pain Strike
The options strike price at which the total dollar value of expiring options (both calls and puts) is minimized — causing the maximum financial loss for option buyers as a group. Calculated by summing the intrinsic value of all open calls and puts at each potential expiry price. Because option sellers (often dealers and institutions) have incentive to pin price near max pain by expiry, this level often acts as a gravitational target during OpEx week, particularly in the last few hours before Friday close.
Market Prep
IV Crush Implied Volatility Collapse
The rapid collapse of implied volatility immediately after a binary event (earnings, FOMC, CPI, product announcements). IV spikes into the event as buyers pay up for protection/speculation, then collapses once the uncertainty resolves — even if the price moves significantly. Options lose value due to vega exposure despite being "right" on direction. Exploited by selling straddles or iron condors before events. The resulting vol crush also triggers vanna flows that can drive systematic buying over the following 2–5 sessions.
Market Prep
OpEx Options Expiration
The date on which options contracts expire. Monthly OpEx = 3rd Friday of each month — the most important, as quarterly and monthly positions all expire simultaneously. Pre-OpEx week (week 3): price pins near large open interest strikes (max pain effect), gamma peaks, vol compresses. Post-OpEx (week 4): all hedges vanish, dealers go gamma-flat, vanna + charm unwinds drive the cleanest directional move of the month. Quarterly OpEx (March, June, September, December) is the largest — also called "triple witching" (stocks + index + ETF options all expire).
Market PrepForecast
0DTE Zero Days to Expiration
Options expiring the same trading day. Now account for >40% of daily SPX options volume. Because gamma spikes as expiry approaches (a 0DTE ATM option can have gamma 10–100× a weekly option), dealer hedging of 0DTE creates powerful intraday price dynamics. Price is magnetically attracted toward high-OI 0DTE strikes as dealers re-hedge continuously. The final 90 minutes of 0DTE trading days often sees the largest moves as charm flow accelerates. Tracked on the 0DTE page and in the Market Prep 0DTE section.
Market PrepForecast
Put Skew
The phenomenon where OTM puts carry higher implied volatility than OTM calls at the same distance from the money. Reflects the crash-risk premium in markets — investors pay more to hedge downside than to speculate on upside. Steep skew = elevated fear (protective put demand high). Flat skew = complacency. Skew inversion (calls > puts IV) = rare melt-up panic buying. Monitored via the IV Surface section of Market Preparation.
Market Prep
Long Straddle
Buy an ATM call and an ATM put with the same strike and expiry. Profits from a large move in either direction — you don't need to know which way, just that vol will be realized. Use when IV Rank is low (cheap to buy) and a catalyst (earnings, FOMC) is expected. Maximum loss = combined premium paid. Best in Short Gamma regimes where realized vol tends to exceed implied.
Market Prep
Calendar Spread Time Spread
Sell a near-term option, buy the same strike at a longer expiry. Profits from near-term IV crush + time decay while maintaining the longer-dated position. Net long vega — benefits when IV rises post-entry. Best use case: after a binary event crushes near-term IV (making the short leg cheaper to buy back), while IV in longer expiries remains elevated. Also used to sell expensive 0DTE premium while hedging with a weekly at the same strike.
Market Prep
Covered Call
Own shares (or a long futures position) and sell an OTM call against the holding. Generates income from the call premium, capping upside above the call strike. Reduces effective cost basis over time. Best in Long Gamma regimes (range-bound markets) when the ML signal is neutral to slightly bullish. Risk: if the stock surges above the short call strike, gains are capped at the strike + premium received.

⚡ Futures Trading
Contract Multiplier
Converts a futures price move (in points) to a dollar gain/loss. Each futures contract has a fixed multiplier: ES=F $50/pt · NQ=F $20/pt · CL=F $1,000/barrel · GC=F $100/oz · SI=F $5,000/oz · ZB=F $1,000/pt. Risk $ = ATR points × Multiplier × 1.5 (stop distance).
Live Trading
ES=F E-mini S&P 500 Futures
CME futures contract tracking the S&P 500 index. Multiplier: $50 per point. One of the most liquid futures markets in the world. Trades nearly 24/5. Represents a bet on the broad US large-cap equity market. Tick size = 0.25 points ($12.50/tick).
Both Pages
NQ=F E-mini Nasdaq-100 Futures
CME futures contract tracking the Nasdaq-100 index. Multiplier: $20 per point. More volatile than ES — higher tech-sector concentration. Used for leveraged tech-focused directional trades. Tick size = 0.25 points ($5/tick).
Both Pages
CL=F WTI Crude Oil Futures
NYMEX futures contract for West Texas Intermediate crude oil. Multiplier: $1,000 per dollar. Highly sensitive to geopolitical events, OPEC decisions, and supply/demand data. One of the highest-volatility futures markets. The ML model uses an oil-specific commodity feature set.
Both Pages
GC=F Gold Futures
COMEX futures for gold. Multiplier: $100 per troy ounce. Moves on dollar strength/weakness, inflation expectations, and geopolitical risk. Acts as a safe-haven asset — rising gold often signals risk-off sentiment. Used by the market summary's World Events engine.
Both Pages
SI=F Silver Futures
COMEX silver futures. Multiplier: $5,000 per troy ounce. Has both industrial and precious-metal characteristics — more volatile than gold and correlated with both commodity cycles and safe-haven demand.
Both Pages
ZB=F 30-Year US Treasury Bond Futures
CBOT futures on the 30-year US Treasury bond. Multiplier: $1,000 per point. Moves inversely to long-term interest rates. Rising ZB = falling long yields (risk-off, flight to safety). The Market Summary World Events engine uses ZB signals to detect Fed policy shifts and monetary policy events.
Both Pages
Contango
A futures market structure where futures prices are higher than the current spot price. Normal for most markets — reflects cost-of-carry (financing, storage). ETFs that roll futures (USO for oil, VIX products) suffer negative roll yield in steep contango — the fund buys expensive far contracts and later sells them cheaper. ES futures typically trade at a slight contango to fair value in normal interest-rate environments.
Market Prep
Backwardation
A futures market structure where futures prices are below the current spot price. Signals either extreme demand pressure or a supply squeeze in the near-term. VIX backwardation (spot VIX > 3-month VIX futures) = near-term fear elevated, historically a reversal precursor within 1–5 sessions. CL backwardation = oil demand spike or supply shock. ETF roll yield is positive in backwardation.
Market Prep
Fair Value ES Futures Fair Value
The theoretical price of ES futures relative to the S&P 500 cash index: Fair Value = SPX × (1 + r×t − d) where r = risk-free rate, t = time to expiry, d = dividend yield. When ES trades above fair value, arbitrageurs buy the cash basket and sell futures (pushing them together). When below fair value, they sell the basket and buy futures. Broadcasted daily by financial media before market open — the "futures premium/discount to fair value" determines the implied cash open price.
Market Prep
COT Data Commitment of Traders
A weekly CFTC report showing the net long/short positioning of Commercial (hedgers), Non-Commercial (speculators), and Non-Reportable (small traders) participants for each futures market. Released every Friday at 3:30pm ET for the prior Tuesday's positions. Extreme net speculative longs (above 80th percentile of historical range) = elevated reversal risk. Extreme net speculative shorts = squeeze potential — look for catalyst. The Market Prep page displays COT proxy bars for ES, NQ, CL, GC, and ZB.

Risk Per Contract
The dollar amount at risk on one futures contract: ATR (in points) × 1.5 × Multiplier. Displayed in the futures trade setup panel. Divide your maximum per-trade dollar risk (2% of account) by this number to determine how many contracts to trade.

🌐 Market Structure & Sentiment
VIX CBOE Volatility Index
The "fear gauge" — measures the market's 30-day implied volatility expectation derived from S&P 500 options prices. VIX < 15 = calm; 15–25 = moderate concern; > 25 = elevated fear; > 30 = high stress. Used by the World Events engine to detect volatility events and geopolitical risk.
Both Pages
Market Breadth
A measure of how many stocks (or assets) are participating in a trend vs. just a few leaders. On the dashboard, computed as the ratio of BUY to SELL signals across all tracked assets. Broad BUY breadth = healthy rally; narrow breadth = only a few assets leading (potential warning sign).

Risk-On / Risk-Off
Risk-on = investors prefer growth assets (stocks, high-yield, commodities). Risk-off = investors flee to safety (bonds, gold, yen, cash). The World Events engine detects regime shifts: SPY+IWM aligned BUY = risk-on; ZB rising + GC rising = risk-off flight to safety.

Safe Haven
Assets that hold or gain value during market stress: Gold (GC=F), US Treasuries (ZB=F), and the US Dollar. When geopolitical or macro risk rises, capital flows into these assets. The dashboard flags a GEOPOLITICAL / SAFE HAVEN event when gold surges without an oil move.

Monetary Policy
Central bank decisions about interest rates and money supply. Detected via ZB=F (Treasury futures) direction: ZB BUY = bond rally = rate-cut expectations (dovish Fed, easing). ZB SELL = bond selloff = rate-hike expectations (hawkish Fed, tightening). Bullish for stocks in easing cycles.

Inflation Signal
Detected when both Gold (GC=F) and Crude Oil (CL=F) are rising simultaneously — a classic dual commodity inflation signal. Rising commodity costs pressure corporate margins and may prompt central banks to raise rates. Classified as BEARISH for equities in the World Events engine.
Market Summary
Market Cap Divergence
When large-cap stocks (SPY) and small-cap stocks (IWM) diverge in direction. SPY BUY + IWM SELL = large-cap leadership without broad participation (potential market top). SPY SELL + IWM BUY = rotation into small caps (risk appetite returning). Detected in the World Events breadth analysis.
Market Summary
Sector Rotation
The movement of institutional capital from one market sector to another as economic conditions shift. At different phases of the economic cycle, different sectors outperform: Early cycle: Financials, Consumer Discretionary, Tech, Real Estate. Mid cycle: Tech, Industrials, Materials. Late cycle: Energy, Materials, Healthcare. Recession: Utilities, Healthcare, Consumer Staples. Detected by tracking 4-week relative outperformance of sector ETFs (XLF, XLK, XLE, XLU, XLP, XLV) vs. SPY.

CTA Commodity Trading Advisors / Trend Followers
Large systematic hedge funds managing ~$350B AUM that use quantitative momentum strategies — they buy what is going up and sell what is going down based on price signals (20/50/100-day breakouts). When CTAs are max long, reversal risk is extreme because there are no new buyers. When CTAs are max short, violent short squeezes occur on any positive catalyst. CTA flip levels (price levels where they switch from long to short) are well-known and create self-fulfilling momentum cascades. Major CTA flip = potential 5–15B daily flow at key S&P price levels.
Market PrepForecast
PFOF Payment for Order Flow
The practice where retail brokers (Robinhood, Schwab, TD Ameritrade) sell their customers' order flow to wholesale market makers (Citadel Securities, Virtu Financial, Susquehanna) who pay a small fee per share for the right to fill orders. The wholesaler profits from the bid-ask spread. Result: 40%+ of retail orders never reach public exchanges — they are internalized. This means retail order flow is "toxic information" to wholesalers, who only internalize it if they can profit. The practice distorts displayed market depth and allows wholesalers to front-run retail sentiment.
Market PrepForecast
Dark Pools Alternative Trading Systems
Private, off-exchange trading venues where large institutions trade without revealing order size or price to the public market until after execution. Account for approximately 35–40% of total US equity volume. Institutions use dark pools to execute large blocks (100K–10M shares) without moving the market. Large dark pool prints that appear on the tape (delayed) signal accumulation or distribution by the largest players — particularly telling in SPY, QQQ, and Mag 7 names. Monitored in the Forecast page's Retail vs. Institutional section.
Market PrepForecast
MOC Imbalance Market-on-Close Imbalance
At 3:50pm ET, NYSE publishes buy/sell imbalances for the Market-on-Close (MOC) auction — the list of stocks where there are more buy than sell orders (or vice versa) for the 4pm closing print. Large buy imbalance (>$500M SPY equivalent) = strong close almost guaranteed; large sell imbalance = weak final 10 minutes. One of the most reliable same-day intraday signals available to traders. Used by market makers and sophisticated traders to position ahead of the close.

Factor Models Factor Investing
Quantitative investment frameworks that explain stock returns through systematic risk factors beyond the market beta: Momentum (12-1 month returns above average tend to persist until sharp reversal — use RSI divergence to time exit). Quality (high ROE + low debt + stable earnings outperforms in late cycle and recessions — MSFT, AAPL, V, JNJ, UNH). Value (low P/E + high dividend yield outperforms when rates peak — BAC, XOM, CVX, WMT, HD). Low Volatility (low-beta stocks outperform in risk-off environments — JNJ, UNH, WMT, XLU, XLP). Covered in the Factor Exposure Guide on the Market Prep page.
Market Prep
Window Dressing
A practice by fund managers at quarter-end (March, June, September, December) of buying recent winners and selling losers so their 13F holdings report looks good to investors. Creates predictable buying pressure on momentum stocks in the last week of each quarter and selling in the first week (as new-quarter repositioning occurs). The first week of the new quarter often reverses the window-dressing effect. Monitored in the Market Prep Institutional Rebalancing Calendar.
Market Prep
Adverse Selection
In market making, the risk that the counterparty in a trade knows more about the true value of the asset than the market maker. When informed traders (institutions with edge) consistently transact with market makers, MMs lose money — they widen the bid-ask spread to compensate. Toxic flow = flow from informed traders that consistently moves against the market maker's position. Retail flow (PFOF) is generally considered non-toxic (non-informed), while dark pool prints are often informed. Wider spreads = higher adverse selection risk detected.

📋 Fundamentals & Asset Analysis
Forward PE Forward Price-to-Earnings Ratio
Current stock price divided by next 12 months' estimated earnings per share (analyst consensus). A valuation multiple measuring how expensive a stock is relative to expected profitability. S&P 500 historical average: ~16–18×. High forward PE (e.g., NVDA 35×+) implies strong growth expectations. Low forward PE (e.g., BAC, XOM 10–13×) implies value or slower growth. Used on the Asset Details hub and in the Year End forecast tab for valuation backdrop analysis.
Asset DetailsForecast
Analyst Rating Fundamental BUY / HOLD / SELL
Wall Street analyst consensus recommendation derived from aggregated price targets and ratings across major banks. BUY = analysts expect >10% upside to target; HOLD = roughly fairly valued, limited catalyst; SELL = analysts expect downside or structural headwinds. Displayed as color-coded chips on Asset Details cards (green BUY, amber HOLD, red SELL). Distinct from the ML model's directional signal — represents medium-term (6–12 month) fundamental view.
Asset Details
Revenue Growth YoY Revenue Growth %
Year-over-year percentage increase in a company's total sales. A key indicator of business momentum and market share. High revenue growth (e.g., NVDA 120%+) justifies high PE multiples during growth phases. Decelerating growth often precedes multiple compression. Combined with earnings growth and margin trends in the fundamentals page to assess whether a stock's ML BUY signal has fundamental confirmation.
Asset DetailsForecast
Net Margin
Net income divided by total revenue — the percentage of sales that becomes profit after all expenses, taxes, and interest. Software/platform businesses (MSFT, META, GOOGL) typically have net margins of 20–40%. Banks report differently (NIM-based). Retail (WMT, HD) and hardware typically have thinner margins (3–10%). High and expanding margins are a quality signal; compressing margins under revenue pressure indicate cost or pricing problems.
Asset DetailsForecast
ROE Return on Equity
Net income divided by shareholders' equity — measures how efficiently management generates profits from equity capital. A Warren Buffett favorite: consistent ROE > 15–20% over a decade signals a durable competitive advantage (moat). Quality factor companies like MSFT, AAPL, V, and UNH maintain high ROE. Used in the Market Prep Factor Exposure Guide to identify quality-factor stocks that outperform in late cycle and recession environments.
Market PrepForecast
Upside % Price Target Upside
The percentage difference between the current stock price and the analyst consensus 12-month price target: (Target − Current) / Current × 100. Upside > 15% = significant analyst conviction; Upside < 5% = near or above consensus target (limited expected return). Displayed in the Fundamentals and Forecast pages. When ML BUY signal + high upside % align, the combined setup has both technical momentum and fundamental support.
Forecast
Market Cap Market Capitalization
Total market value of a company's outstanding shares: Share Price × Shares Outstanding. Mega-cap (>$1T): AAPL, MSFT, NVDA, GOOGL, AMZN, META. Large-cap ($10B–$1T): most S&P 500 members. Small-cap (<$2B): Russell 2000 (IWM). Larger market caps generally mean lower volatility, better liquidity, more analyst coverage, and higher index fund ownership — but also less potential for explosive growth.
Asset DetailsForecast
Fund Score Fundamental Score
A composite 0–100 score computed from multiple fundamental metrics: analyst rating, forward PE relative to sector, revenue growth, net margin, ROE, and price-to-target upside. Score > 70 = fundamentally strong stock worth BUY signal confirmation. Score < 30 = fundamentally challenged — SELL signals have additional support. Displayed as a progress bar on the Fundamentals page and the Asset Details hub cards.

⏱ Timeframes & Intervals
1m 1 Minute
The shortest available chart interval — each bar covers one minute of trading. Max history: ~5 days (yfinance limit). Best for intraday scalping. Note: ML signals are computed on 1H bars regardless of the chart timeframe — the signal arrows will still show at their correct timestamps.
Live Trading
5m 5 Minute
Each bar covers 5 minutes of trading. Max history: ~30 days. Common for short-term intraday trading. Provides more context than 1m while still showing intraday structure. Good for timing entries after the 1H ML signal triggers.
Live Trading
15m 15 Minute
Each bar covers 15 minutes. Max history: ~60 days. Balances intraday detail with broader context. Useful for seeing swing structures and support/resistance levels that inform stop and target placement within the 1H signal framework.
Live Trading
30m 30 Minute
Each bar covers 30 minutes. Max history: ~60 days. Shows half-session structure — morning and afternoon sessions are clearly separated. Useful for identifying intraday trends and key pivot levels that align with ML signal direction.
Live Trading
1H 1 Hour — Default
The default chart interval and the primary timeframe the ML models are trained on. Each bar = one hour of trading. History: 90 days (~1,700 bars). The ML signal, P(UP), magnitude, stop, and target are all calibrated to this interval. Signal markers align perfectly with 1H candles.
Both Pages
4H 4 Hour
Four-hour bars, constructed by resampling 1H data (OHLCV aggregation). History: 90 days (~420 bars). A popular swing-trading timeframe that filters out intraday noise. The ML models can be applied at this timeframe for positions held 1–3 days.
Both Pages
1D 1 Day
Daily bars — each candle represents one full trading session. History: 365 days. Used for the Multi-Horizon Forecast models (1-day, 1-week, 1-month predictions). Daily charts reveal the primary trend that shorter timeframes should align with.
Both Pages
Multi-Horizon Forecast
Separate GBM models trained on daily data to forecast returns over 1 Day, 1 Week (5 trading days), and 1 Month (20 trading days). Each horizon has its own P(UP), magnitude %, and target price. Available for SPY, QQQ, ES=F, GC=F on the Market Summary page.

🔌 Platform & Data Sources
IBKR Interactive Brokers
The recommended broker for this dashboard — supports stocks, options, and futures from a single account. Provides real-time market data when TWS or IB Gateway is running and connected. Connects via the ib_async Python library on port 7497 (paper) or 7496 (live).
Live Trading
TWS Trader Workstation
Interactive Brokers' main desktop trading application. Must be running and logged in with API access enabled for the dashboard to receive real-time data. Paper trading port: 7497. Live trading port: 7496. Enable via TWS → File → Global Configuration → API → Settings.
Live Trading
IB Gateway
A lightweight alternative to TWS that runs headlessly (no GUI). Uses the same ports (7497 paper, 7496 live). Preferred for server deployments where TWS's full interface is unnecessary. Connects the same way via ib_async.
Live Trading
yfinance
A Python library that downloads 15–20 minute delayed historical market data from Yahoo Finance. Used as the fallback data source when IBKR is not connected. Free and requires no API key. Supports 1m through 1d intervals with various history limits per interval.
Both Pages
Real-Time Data
Live market prices with millisecond or second latency, available when connected to IBKR via TWS or IB Gateway. Shown as ● IBKR Live in the status badge. Required for active intraday trading where delayed data would result in stale signals.
Live Trading
Delayed Data
Market data that is 15–20 minutes behind real time — provided by yfinance when IBKR is not connected. Shown as ● yfinance in the status badge. Sufficient for educational purposes and end-of-day swing trading analysis, but not suitable for intraday execution.
Both Pages
In-Memory Cache 4-Min TTL
The Flask server stores computed ML signals and candle data in memory for 4 minutes (240 seconds). Subsequent requests within the TTL return instantly without re-running the ML model. The ↻ Refresh button bypasses the cache (?nocache=1) to force a fresh computation.
Both Pages
Paper Trading
Simulated trading with virtual money — no real capital at risk. IBKR offers a paper trading account on port 7497. Useful for testing the ML signals and building confidence before committing real capital. All features of the dashboard work identically in paper mode.
Live Trading
live-server.py
The Python Flask backend server that powers both dashboards. Provides REST API endpoints: /api/candles/<symbol>, /api/signal/<symbol>, /api/market-summary, /api/forecast/<symbol>, /api/ibkr-status. Run with python live-server.py on port 5050.

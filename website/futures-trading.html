⚡ Why Futures Are the Professional's Instrument

Futures contracts are standardized agreements to buy or sell an underlying asset at a fixed price on a future date. Unlike stocks, futures offer built-in leverage, 24-hour liquidity, and the ability to go short without a margin account or uptick rules. Professional traders and hedge funds use futures to express directional views with capital efficiency that stocks and options cannot match.

Machine learning models applied to futures face a different challenge than stock pickers: the signal-to-noise ratio is lower, leverage amplifies both wins and losses, and roll costs must be factored into every position. Getting the ML edge right is therefore worth more — because each correctly predicted move is magnified by leverage.

🔧 ML Feature Engineering for Futures

Futures markets respond to a different mix of signals than equities. The feature vector must capture both technical price action and the fundamental drivers unique to each market.

Trend & Momentum

SMA(20/50/200), EMA crossovers, RSI(14), MACD signal, Rate of Change. Universal across all futures markets.

Volatility

ATR(14), Bollinger Band width, VIX level and term structure slope (for ES/NQ), 20-day realized vol, overnight range gap.

Macro / Fundamental

EIA inventory reports (CL), Fed funds rate changes (ES/GC), USD index (GC/CL), COT report positioning, sector rotation flows.

Market Structure

Open interest changes, roll spread (front vs. back month), put/call ratio on futures options, basis vs. fair value, day-of-week and seasonality.

Futures-Specific Features That Improve ML Accuracy

  • Open interest trend: Rising OI on a price advance confirms institutional accumulation — stronger bullish signal than price alone.
  • Roll spread: The difference between front-month and next-month prices (contango vs. backwardation) reveals supply/demand pressure.
  • COT commercial positioning: Commitments of Traders report shows what commercial hedgers (producers, refiners) are doing — often a contrarian signal at extremes.
  • Overnight/globex range: Large overnight moves that fail to hold during regular trading hours predict mean reversion — an important intraday feature for day-trading models.

Why 61.8% Is Highly Profitable with Leverage

Consider 100 ES trades with the confidence filter, risking 1.5× ATR (~$875/contract stop):

  • 62 winners average +$1,450/contract (1.66× ATR move captured)
  • 38 losers average −$875/contract (stopped at 1.5× ATR)
  • Expectancy: (0.618 × $1,450) + (0.382 × −$875) = +$562 per trade
  • 100 trades × $562 = $56,200 per contract per year on $12,000 margin

📐 Magnitude Prediction — Trade Economics

Gradient Boosting Regressor Output

For today's high-confidence ES signal (P(UP)=0.71), the magnitude regressor outputs:

  • Point estimate: +41 ES points (+0.82%)
  • 90th-percentile interval: +11 to +71 ES points
  • Even at the lower bound (+11 pts), profit = 11 × $50 = $550/contract
  • At point estimate (+41 pts), profit = 41 × $50 = $2,050/contract

Comparing Magnitude to Risk

ATR(14) for ES = 28 points. Stop placed at 1.5× ATR = 42 points below entry. Risk per contract = 42 × $50 = $2,100.

  • Risk/Reward at point estimate: $2,050 profit / $2,100 risk = 0.98R
  • Risk/Reward at 90th pct upper: $3,550 / $2,100 = 1.69R
  • Expected value: 0.618 × (average win) − 0.382 × $2,100 = positive when win > $1,297

The magnitude model confirms the trade has positive expected value even if the move is modest. Trades where the point estimate delivers less than 1× ATR of profit are skipped — insufficient reward for the risk taken.

🚦 Signal Checklist and Entry Rules

A valid ES long signal requires ALL of the following conditions:

  • ✅ XGBoost P(UP) ≥ 0.65 (high-confidence direction signal)
  • ✅ Magnitude forecast ≥ 1.0× ATR (sufficient reward-to-risk)
  • ✅ VIX below 25 (panic regimes suppress signal quality)
  • ✅ Not within 2 trading days of FOMC meeting (policy surprise risk)
  • ✅ Open interest not declining more than 5% (declining OI weakens confirmation)
  • ✅ Trend filter: price above 20-day SMA (trade in direction of intermediate trend)

Entry Timing

ML signals are generated on the closing bar. Entry is placed on the next session's open using a limit order 2–3 ticks below the opening print to avoid paying the full gap premium. If not filled within the first 30 minutes of regular trading hours, cancel and wait for the next signal. Forcing entry at a worse price destroys the edge.

Managing a Position That Goes Against You

If ES falls to 4,800 before target, the stop executes automatically. Loss = 42 × $50 = $2,100. This is not a failure — it is the plan working. The Kelly Criterion ensures that even with 38% losing trades, the portfolio compounds positively when winners are systematically larger. Never move the stop further away to avoid taking a loss.

📊 Futures Risk Mathematics

Leverage and Position Sizing

Futures leverage is implicit — you do not choose it separately from the position. Position size must be calculated from risk dollars, not from margin dollars:

Contracts = (Account × Risk%) / (ATR × Multiplier × ATR_multiple)

Example: $50,000 account, 2% risk = $1,000. ATR = 28 pts, multiplier = $50, stop = 1.5× ATR:

Contracts = $1,000 / (28 × $50 × 1.5) = $1,000 / $2,100 = 0.48 → 0 contracts

This account size cannot safely trade ES with 2% risk per trade. Minimum account for 1 ES contract at 2% risk: $2,100 / 0.02 = $105,000. Smaller accounts should use Micro E-mini (MES) contracts, which are 1/10th the size of ES.

Value at Risk (VaR) — 95% Confidence

VaR(95%) = Portfolio × Daily_Vol × 1.645

For 1 ES contract at $245,000 notional with 1% daily volatility (25 pts): VaR = $245,000 × 0.01 × 1.645 = $4,030 at 95% confidence. This means on the worst 5% of days, expect to lose at least $4,030 per contract. Size accordingly.

Kelly Criterion for Futures

f* = (p·b − q) / b → half-Kelly = f*/2

ES backtest: p = 0.618, average win = $1,450, average loss = $875 → b = 1,450/875 = 1.66. f* = (0.618 × 1.66 − 0.382) / 1.66 = 0.388 = 38.8% of equity per trade. Half-Kelly = 19.4%. With a $50,000 account: $9,700 per trade. Since ES margin is $12,000, the Kelly constraint effectively limits to 1 contract on this account.

🛢 Crude Oil (CL) — Inventory-Driven ML

Fundamental Features for CL

Crude oil is the most fundamentally-driven major futures market. The ML feature vector extends beyond technicals:

  • EIA Weekly Inventory Report: Actual vs. consensus surprise in millions of barrels. A draw of 3Mb+ versus a consensus draw of 1Mb = strong bullish shock.
  • Baker Hughes Rig Count: Weekly US oil rig count trend. Rising rigs signal future supply growth — bearish 6–12 months forward.
  • Crack Spread (321): Refinery profitability = (2 × gasoline + 1 × heating oil − 3 × crude) / 3. Rising crack spread signals strong refined product demand → bullish crude.
  • USD Index: Oil is priced in dollars. Strong USD → cheaper for foreign buyers but strong dollar typically inversely correlated with crude price.
  • OPEC meeting proximity: Binary feature: 1 if within 10 days of an OPEC+ meeting. These meetings create outsized volatility and regime shifts.

🥇 Gold (GC) — LSTM-Driven Macro Model

Why Gold Needs LSTM

Gold responds to overlapping macro cycles spanning weeks to months: real interest rates, geopolitical risk, central bank demand, and USD strength. A static feature vector misses how these factors compound over time. A 2-layer LSTM processes 60-day sequences of:

  • 10-year real yield (nominal yield minus breakeven inflation)
  • DXY (Dollar Index) level and 20-day momentum
  • Gold ETF flows (GLD) — daily share creation/redemption
  • VIX (flight-to-safety demand proxy)
  • Central bank buying data (monthly, interpolated daily)

🔄 Contract Rolling — Backwardation vs. Contango

Futures contracts expire. Traders who want continuous exposure must roll their position from the expiring front month to the next active month. The roll spread (front minus back) reveals the market's supply/demand balance:

Backwardation

Front month priced higher than back months. Indicates current supply tightness. Rolling costs money (sell high, buy lower back month = roll gain). Bullish for the commodity.

Contango

Front month priced lower than back months. Normal condition for storable commodities; storage cost baked in. Rolling costs money (sell low, buy higher back month = roll loss).

Roll Timing Rule

Roll ES/NQ at least 5 trading days before expiration (first notice day for commodity futures may be earlier). Rolling too late exposes you to thin liquidity and wide bid-ask spreads in the expiring contract. Always check roll dates on the CME Group calendar.

⚠️ Critical Futures Trading Warnings

1. Margin Calls Destroy Accounts

If your account drops below the maintenance margin level (typically 75% of initial margin), you receive a margin call — you must deposit more funds immediately or the broker will close your position at the current market price. This forced liquidation can happen at the worst possible moment. Never let your account approach maintenance margin. Rule: Never use more than 50% of account equity for initial margin.

2. News Events Create Unfillable Gaps

Futures can gap far beyond your stop-loss price during major news releases (NFP, FOMC, geopolitical events). A 42-point ES stop becomes ineffective if ES gaps 80 points overnight. This is called slippage risk. Mitigate by avoiding overnight holds through major scheduled announcements, and by using options on futures to cap maximum loss when holding overnight.

3. Leverage Amplifies Model Errors

A ML model with 61.8% accuracy sounds excellent — but a streak of 5 consecutive losing trades loses 5 × $875 = $4,375 on ES. With a $12,000 account using 1 contract, this is a 36% drawdown. Always size so that a 10-trade losing streak does not reduce your account below 50% of starting equity. If it would, you are trading too large.

📖 Why Machine Learning in Trading?

Financial markets generate enormous amounts of structured data — prices, volumes, order flow, earnings, and macroeconomic indicators — across thousands of instruments and decades of history. Human traders can analyze only a fraction of this information at a time. Machine learning models have no such constraint.

ML approaches markets as a pattern recognition problem: given the history of a price series and derived features, predict future price direction or magnitude. The patterns ML discovers are often non-linear, multi-dimensional, and time-varying — beyond the reach of traditional technical or fundamental analysis.

Leading quantitative funds — Renaissance Technologies, Two Sigma, D.E. Shaw, and Citadel — use sophisticated ML pipelines that have generated billions in alpha over decades. This module introduces the core concepts behind these systems.

🗄 Market Data: The S&P 500 Dataset

The S&P 500 is the most widely tracked equity index in the world — a cap-weighted basket of 500 large US companies. It provides a clean, survivorship-adjusted benchmark with decades of daily price data.

OHLCV Data Fields

Each trading session produces five data points:

  • Open — Price of the first trade when the market opens at 9:30 AM ET.
  • High — Highest price reached during the session.
  • Low — Lowest price reached during the session.
  • Close — Price of the last trade at 4:00 PM ET. The most widely used reference price.
  • Volume — Total shares traded. High volume on a price move confirms significance.

Why OHLCV Is Rich in Information

The spread between high and low (the session range) reveals intraday volatility. The relationship between open and close (the candle body) indicates directional conviction. Volume relative to its moving average signals unusual institutional activity.

Data Preprocessing Considerations

  • Adjusting for splits & dividends — Raw prices must be backward-adjusted to be comparable across time.
  • Returns vs. prices — Log returns (ln(Pₜ/Pₜ₋₁)) are stationary and more suitable for ML models than raw prices.
  • Normalization — Rolling z-score normalization makes features comparable across different market regimes.
  • Stationarity — Many ML models assume stationary inputs. Raw prices are non-stationary; returns and detrended series are more suitable.

🔧 Feature Engineering for Markets

Raw OHLCV data contains little predictive signal on its own. The power of ML in trading comes largely from feature engineering — transforming raw price action into informative indicators that expose the underlying market structure.

Trend Features (Moving Averages)

  • SMA(20) — Simple 20-day moving average. Smooths noise; the current price relative to SMA-20 measures short-term trend.
  • SMA(50) — 50-day moving average. Medium-term trend indicator. A golden cross (SMA-20 crossing above SMA-50) is a classic bullish signal.
  • EMA(12/26) — Exponential moving averages weight recent prices more. Form the basis of MACD.
  • Price/SMA ratio — How far price has deviated from its moving average. Mean reversion signal.

Momentum Features

  • RSI (14-day) — Relative Strength Index. Oscillates between 0 and 100. Above 70 = overbought; below 30 = oversold. One of the most reliable short-term mean-reversion signals.
  • MACD — Moving Average Convergence Divergence. Difference between EMA(12) and EMA(26). Signal line is EMA(9) of MACD. Crossovers indicate momentum shifts.
  • Rate of Change (ROC) — Percentage price change over N days. Captures medium-term momentum.
  • Williams %R — Measures where current price sits within the N-day high-low range. Similar to RSI but inverted.

Volatility Features

  • Bollinger Bands — Upper and lower bands at SMA ± 2σ (two standard deviations). Price touching the lower band signals potential reversal; breakouts confirm momentum.
  • ATR (Average True Range) — Average of the true range (max of: High−Low, |High−prev.Close|, |Low−prev.Close|) over N days. Quantifies recent volatility for position sizing.
  • Rolling Standard Deviation — 20-day standard deviation of log returns. Regime indicator — high volatility precedes large moves.

Volume Features

  • On-Balance Volume (OBV) — Running total of volume with sign determined by price direction. Confirms price trends when OBV aligns.
  • VWAP — Volume-Weighted Average Price. Institutions measure execution quality relative to VWAP.
  • Volume Ratio — Today's volume divided by 20-day average. >2 signals unusual activity.

Derived Statistical Features

  • Rolling skewness and kurtosis of returns (market regime detection)
  • Autocorrelation of returns (measures persistence)
  • Intraday range as a percentage of close (relative volatility)
  • Gap: today's open vs. yesterday's close (overnight risk)

🎯 Predicting Market Direction (Classification)

The most common ML trading formulation: predict whether the S&P 500 will close higher (+1) or lower (−1) tomorrow, given today's feature vector.

y = sign(Close[t+1] − Close[t]) ∈ {+1, −1}

Algorithm: Random Forest Classifier

Random Forests are particularly well-suited to this problem because:

  • They handle non-linear, high-dimensional feature spaces without overfitting as severely as individual trees
  • They are robust to noisy, non-stationary data
  • They provide feature importance rankings, revealing which indicators contribute most
  • They require minimal hyperparameter tuning to achieve competitive performance

Confusion Matrix Interpretation

For a binary direction classifier, four outcomes are possible:

True Positive (TP)

Predicted UP and market went UP. Profitable long trade.

True Negative (TN)

Predicted DOWN and market went DOWN. Profitable short trade.

False Positive (FP)

Predicted UP but market went DOWN. Long position loses money.

False Negative (FN)

Predicted DOWN but market went UP. Missed opportunity or short loses.

Realistic Performance Expectations

Markets are near-efficient — directional accuracy of 52–58% is considered strong. A 55% accuracy model, applied consistently with proper position sizing, can generate significant risk-adjusted returns over thousands of trades. The edge is statistical, not certain on any individual trade.

📐 Predicting Move Magnitude (Regression)

Knowing direction is valuable. Knowing how much the market will move is even more powerful — it enables proportional position sizing. When the model is highly confident of a large move, you invest more; when confidence is low, you reduce exposure.

y = (Close[t+1] − Close[t]) / Close[t] ← next-day log return

Prediction Intervals

Rather than a single point estimate, advanced models output a prediction interval — upper and lower bounds around the expected return. These bounds can be estimated via:

  • Quantile regression — Directly models the Nth percentile of the return distribution
  • Conformal prediction — Distribution-free, guaranteed coverage of true value with probability 1−α
  • Bayesian neural networks — Maintain uncertainty estimates over weights, propagating uncertainty to predictions

Position Sizing from Magnitude Forecasts

The Kelly Criterion provides a theoretically optimal position size as a fraction of portfolio:

f* = (p·b − q) / b

where p = win probability, q = 1−p, b = win/loss ratio. In practice, "half Kelly" (f*/2) is used to reduce variance while still compounding efficiently.

🔁 Long Short-Term Memory Networks

Standard feedforward networks treat each day's feature vector independently. But markets have temporal structure — a sequence of 20 days of data tells a richer story than 20 isolated daily snapshots. LSTMs are designed to exploit this structure.

How LSTM Memory Works

An LSTM cell maintains two state vectors — the cell state (long-term memory) and the hidden state (working memory). Three learned gates control information flow:

  • Forget Gate — Decides what fraction of the current cell state to discard. Sigmoid output 0 = forget everything, 1 = retain everything.
  • Input Gate — Decides what new information to write into the cell state.
  • Output Gate — Decides what portion of the cell state to expose as the hidden state (output).
Cell state: C[t] = f·C[t-1] + i·tanh(W_c·[h[t-1], x[t]] + b_c)
Hidden state: h[t] = o·tanh(C[t])

LSTM Architecture for Trading

  • Input sequence: 20–60 trading days of features (OHLCV + derived indicators)
  • LSTM layers: 1–3 stacked layers with 64–256 hidden units each
  • Dropout: 0.2–0.4 between LSTM layers for regularization
  • Output: Dense layer with 1 unit (regression) or softmax (classification)

Why LSTM Outperforms Simple Models on Time Series

LSTM can learn that a pullback after a 3-day rally in a rising-rate environment predicts a bounce — a relationship spanning multiple days that a static feature vector would miss. The gating mechanism allows the network to selectively remember relevant historical context.

📋 Backtesting & Performance Metrics

Backtesting simulates how a trading strategy would have performed on historical data. It is the primary tool for evaluating ML trading models before risking real capital.

Critical Pitfalls

  • Look-ahead bias — Using data that would not have been available at decision time. Example: using tomorrow's open price as a feature. This is the most common and dangerous error.
  • Survivorship bias — Only testing on stocks still trading today. Companies that went bankrupt are excluded, inflating apparent returns.
  • Overfitting — Tuning strategy parameters to fit historical data specifically. Always use a held-out test period the model was never exposed to.
  • Transaction costs — Commissions, bid-ask spread, and market impact. A strategy showing 15% annual gross return may deliver only 8% net.

Walk-Forward Analysis

The gold standard for time series validation. Train on data from years 1–5, test on year 6. Slide forward: train on years 2–6, test on year 7. Repeat. This mimics the true deployment environment and reveals whether the strategy generalizes across different market regimes.

Performance Metrics

Total Return

Cumulative portfolio gain over the backtest period. Compare to buy-and-hold benchmark.

CAGR

Compound Annual Growth Rate. Annualized return accounting for compounding.

Sharpe Ratio

(Return − Risk-free rate) / Volatility. >1 = good, >1.5 = excellent, >2 = exceptional.

Max Drawdown

Largest peak-to-trough decline. Measures worst-case loss from a portfolio peak.

Calmar Ratio

CAGR / Max Drawdown. Risk-adjusted return relative to the worst loss experienced.

Win Rate

Percentage of trades that are profitable. Must be interpreted alongside win/loss ratio.

🛡 Risk Management

Even a profitable strategy can be ruined by poor risk management. Protecting capital is the prerequisite for long-term compounding.

  • Position sizing — Never risk more than 1–2% of portfolio on a single trade. Use ATR-based sizing to adjust for current volatility.
  • Stop-loss orders — Exit trades that move against you beyond a predefined threshold. Protects against rare large adverse moves.
  • Volatility targeting — Scale position sizes inversely with realized volatility to maintain constant portfolio risk across market regimes.
  • Diversification — Trade multiple uncorrelated instruments or strategies to reduce idiosyncratic risk.
  • Maximum drawdown limits — Automatically reduce position sizes or halt trading if drawdown exceeds a threshold (e.g., 15%). Prevents the strategy from destroying capital in adverse regimes.
  • Regime detection — Identify whether the current market is trending, mean-reverting, or in crisis mode, and adjust strategy parameters accordingly.

🔑 Why Options Require Three ML Predictions

Buying a stock requires one prediction: direction. Options require three: direction, magnitude, and implied volatility. An option purchased when IV is at 80th percentile will lose money even on a correct directional prediction if IV collapses after entry. ML systems that predict all three simultaneously hold a structural edge over directional traders.

📚 Options Fundamentals

Calls vs. Puts

  • Call option: Right to BUY 100 shares at the strike price. Profits when the underlying rises above strike by expiration. AAPL 185 call profits if AAPL > $185 by expiry.
  • Put option: Right to SELL 100 shares at strike price. Profits when the underlying falls below strike. AAPL 185 put profits if AAPL < $185 by expiry.

Premium Components

  • Intrinsic value: How far in-the-money the option is. An AAPL 183 call when AAPL = 185 has $2.00 of intrinsic value.
  • Time value (extrinsic): The premium above intrinsic value, driven by time remaining and implied volatility. Decays to zero at expiration.
  • Implied Volatility (IV): The market's forecast of future volatility embedded in the premium. High IV = expensive options. Low IV = cheap options.

Implied Volatility Rank (IV Rank)

IV Rank = (Current IV − 52-week low IV) / (52-week high IV − 52-week low IV) × 100

IV Rank > 60 = expensive options (sell premium). IV Rank < 30 = cheap options (buy premium). This single metric is the most valuable filter in ML-driven options systems.

🔢 The Greeks — Measuring Options Risk

Delta (Δ)

Price sensitivity. A 0.50 delta call gains $50 in value for each $1 rise in the underlying stock. Delta for calls: 0 to 1. For puts: −1 to 0.

Gamma (Γ)

Delta acceleration. Rate of delta change per $1 move. Highest at the money near expiration. Long options have positive gamma — directional gains accelerate.

Theta (Θ)

Time decay. Daily premium erosion. An AAPL 185 call with 7 DTE decays ~$15/day. Time works against long options buyers, for sellers.

Vega (ν)

IV sensitivity. A vega of 0.15 means the option gains $15 for each 1-point rise in IV. Long options have positive vega — IV expansion is profitable.

Managing Greeks in Practice

For a bull call spread with delta 0.31 and theta −$8/day:

  • After 3 days flat, time decay consumes 3 × $8 = $24 of the $142 premium paid (16.9%).
  • Closing at 50% of maximum profit ($79 gain) after only 2 days preserves capital from theta erosion.
  • Never hold short options through expiration — gamma accelerates and the $142 invested can go to zero rapidly.

🤖 The Dual ML System: Direction + IV

XGBoost Direction Classifier

The same XGBoost architecture used for stocks, retrained on SPY with additional options-specific features: 30-day realized volatility, term structure slope, and pre-earnings distance. For today's AAPL: P(UP) = 0.68, predicted magnitude = +1.8%.

LSTM Implied Volatility Forecaster

A 2-layer LSTM processes 60-day sequences of: VIX level, VIX term structure slope, realized vs. implied volatility spread, and put/call skew. It outputs a probability that IV will expand or contract over the next 5 days.

  • IV direction accuracy: 61%
  • Mean absolute error: 2.3 volatility points
  • Contribution to edge: +8.9% win rate improvement vs. direction-only system

Why Magnitude Determines Strike Selection

An AAPL 185 call with 7 DTE requires a 2.1% move to break even. With a predicted magnitude of only +1.8%, this call has negative expected value — you'd be buying an option that needs a bigger move than the model forecasts. The bull call spread 185/188 lowers break-even to 0.8%, well within the prediction interval.

⚡ Executing the Bull Call Spread

Today's AAPL Trade

Signal: P(UP)=0.68, magnitude +1.8%, IV rank 35% → Bull Call Spread

  • Buy: AAPL 185 Call at $2.84
  • Sell: AAPL 188 Call at ($1.42)
  • Net debit: $1.42 per share = $142 per contract
  • Maximum profit: $1.58 per share = $158 per contract (at $188+)
  • Maximum loss: $1.42 per share = $142 per contract (total premium paid)
  • Break-even: $186.42 (requires only +0.8% from $185.20)

Order Entry

Submit as a net debit limit order at $1.42 (the mid-price between bid and ask for the spread). Never use a market order for options — spreads can be wide and market orders fill at the worst possible price. For a $10,000 account, max 2% risk = $200 → 1 contract at $142 debit.

Mechanical Exit Rules

  • Take profit at 50% of max ($79 gain): The spread reaches $2.21 — close immediately. Locking in 50% eliminates the final 50% risk while keeping most of the expected value.
  • Stop at 50% of premium paid ($71 loss): Spread drops to $0.71 — close immediately. This rule keeps average losses below $71 and average wins above $79.
  • Time rule: Close all spreads by DTE 2 (two days before expiration) regardless of P&L. Gamma risk accelerates and spreads can move violently in final hours.

🛡 Disciplined Loss Management

Options can expire at zero. The difference between a profitable options trader and a losing one is almost entirely about how losses are managed.

Long Premium (Bought Options/Debit Spreads)

  • Close at 50% of premium paid if the underlying moves against you within 3 days
  • This preserves 50% of capital for the next signal — which is better than holding and letting time decay erase everything
  • Never average down by buying more of a losing options position

Short Premium (Credit Spreads, Iron Condors)

  • Close at 200% of premium received if the underlying breaches your short strike
  • A $1.42 credit that turns into a $3.00 loss can happen fast — act at 2× rule, no exceptions
  • Never hold losing short options to expiration hoping for recovery — defined-risk spreads prevent the worst, but max loss is still real

The golden rule: Pre-define every exit before you enter. Write it down. Execute mechanically. Options trading is won or lost not on entry quality but on exit discipline.

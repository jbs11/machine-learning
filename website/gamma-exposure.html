SpotGamma Concepts Explained
Gamma Exposure (GEX) measures the net dollar gamma that market makers (dealers) must hedge across all options strikes. When dealers are net long gamma, they sell rallies and buy dips — stabilizing price around key strikes (like the famous "gamma squeeze" in reverse). When dealers are net short gamma, they must chase the market — amplifying moves in both directions. GEX is calculated as: Net GEX = (Call γ × Call OI − Put γ × Put OI) × 100 × Spot where γ is Black-Scholes gamma computed from each strike's implied volatility.
🟢
Gamma Wall (Call Wall)
The strike with the highest positive net GEX. Market makers are most aggressively hedging here — they'll sell into any rally that approaches this level and buy on any dip away from it. Acts as a strong magnet, pulling price toward it and resisting breakouts.
Above spot → Strong resistanceBelow spot → Strong support
🔴
Put Wall
The strike with the highest negative net GEX — typically where heavy put open interest concentrates. Dealers who sold puts must buy underlying when price approaches this level (delta-hedging). Creates mechanical buying pressure; often a key support floor during selloffs.
High put OI = dealer buying floorBounce zone in corrections
🟣
Gamma Flip Level
The strike where cumulative GEX transitions from positive to negative (or vice versa). Above the flip: market is in long-gamma (stabilizing) territory. Below the flip: short-gamma (trending) territory. Crossing the Gamma Flip is the most important level to watch — it signals a regime change in realized volatility.
Above flip → Lower vol, range-boundBelow flip → Higher vol, trending
⚖️
Long vs Short Gamma Regime
When total net GEX is positive (Long Gamma), dealers act as vol-sellers — dampening moves and keeping realized vol below implied vol. When net GEX is negative (Short Gamma), dealers become forced buyers/sellers at inopportune times — realized vol rises and trending moves extend further than expected.
Long Gamma → Mean-reversionShort Gamma → Trending/volatile
📊
Put/Call Ratio (PCR)
Open interest in puts divided by open interest in calls. PCR >1.0 means more puts than calls (bearish hedging dominates). PCR <0.7 is bullish (call buying dominates). Extreme values often act as contrarian signals — very high PCR can signal a sentiment bottom; very low PCR can signal complacency.
PCR >1.2 Bearish hedgingPCR <0.7 Bullish sentiment
🔢
How to Read the Chart
Strikes are on the Y-axis (high at top, low at bottom). GEX dollar value on the X-axis. Green bars extend right = positive GEX (stabilizing). Red bars extend left = negative GEX (destabilizing). The wider the bar, the more gamma concentration at that strike. All values computed from front-month options expiring within 60 days.
Wide green = strong support/resistWide red = vol catalyst zone

📘 Options Flow Concepts
🌊
What is Options Flow?
Options flow tracks the volume and premium dollars traded in the options market per strike and expiry. Large institutional orders — often called "smart money" — leave footprints in the flow data. Calls-heavy flow typically signals bullish positioning; puts-heavy flow signals hedging or bearish conviction.
🌀
Tornado Chart
The tornado chart displays per-strike options volume: calls extend right (green) and puts extend left (red). Each horizontal bar represents a strike price. Wide green bars at strikes above spot suggest call buying; wide red bars below spot suggest put accumulation. The yellow line marks current spot price.
⚡
0DTE (Zero Days to Expiry)
0DTE options expire the same day they are traded. These ultra-short-dated contracts are dominated by retail day traders and institutional gamma scalpers. High 0DTE call volume at a strike above spot signals intraday bullish momentum bets. Heavy 0DTE put volume near current spot suggests hedging or downside speculation for the same session.
🚨
Unusual Activity
An options trade is flagged unusual when volume exceeds 3× open interest AND volume > 500 contracts. This pattern indicates fresh positioning — the contracts weren't already open — which is the hallmark of a new institutional directional bet. The vol/OI ratio (shown as "×OI") measures how much stronger today's flow is versus existing positions.
📊
Put/Call Ratio
The Put/Call (P/C) ratio compares put volume or premium to calls. P/C vol < 0.8 = bullish skew; P/C vol > 1.2 = bearish skew. The premium-based P/C is more reliable: heavy put premium can indicate institutional downside hedging even when put volume is lower than calls, because large puts are more expensive per contract.
💵
Net Premium (Call$ − Put$)
Net premium per strike = total call dollar premium − total put dollar premium at that strike. Positive (green) means more call money flowed into that strike — bullish. Negative (red) means more put premium — bearish. Clusters of positive net premium above spot are the strongest bullish signals; negative clusters below spot signal strong hedging demand.
🔄
Delta Hedging Mechanics
When a market maker sells an option, they take on directional risk (delta). To remain market-neutral, they immediately buy or sell the underlying stock proportional to the option's delta. Selling calls → dealers buy stock (bullish pressure). Selling puts → dealers sell stock (bearish pressure). The Dealer Delta Hedge Pressure chart shows the estimated dollar value of stock dealers must transact at each strike, based on Black-Scholes delta weighted by traded volume.
🚀
Gamma Squeeze
Gamma is the rate of change of delta. When price moves toward a strike with heavy call open interest, the delta of those calls increases — forcing dealers to buy more stock to maintain their hedge. This accelerating buy demand creates a self-reinforcing rally: higher price → higher delta → more dealer buying → even higher price. The most violent squeezes occur when dealers are short large amounts of near-expiry calls with the spot price approaching the strike. 🔺 Squeeze badges identify assets at risk.
📍
Max Pain & Pin Risk
Max pain is the strike price at which the total dollar value of expiring options (calls + puts) reaches its minimum — i.e., where option buyers collectively lose the most. Since dealers and market makers are net short options, price gravitating to max pain maximizes their profit. The purple line on the dealer delta chart marks this level. Pin risk describes the tendency for price to be "pinned" at high-OI strikes near expiry as dealers repeatedly hedge and de-hedge, dampening volatility near expiry.
🏦
Institutional Pressure & Direction
Institutional options orders are rarely about pure speculation — they reflect portfolio hedging, structured product creation, and risk management. Large call sweeps above spot from institutional desks create systematic dealer buy pressure. Large put blocks suggest institutions are hedging long equity exposure, creating sell pressure. The combination of flow sentiment, net dealer delta, unusual activity ratios, and premium skew gives the most reliable read on institutional directional conviction and expected price movement magnitude.
